Common Mistakes in Transport Tracking Implementation and How to Avoid Them

Vehicle tracking systems are attaining traction in India, meeting efficiency and safety in transportation. Yet, the road to successful implementation is full of obstacles. Common mistakes can lead to several challenges and unexpected costs.

This blog guides you through implementing a vehicle tracking system, highlighting the essential do's and don'ts to avoid common pitfalls.

What is Transport Tracking?

Transport tracking is a user-friendly software for monitoring vehicles and shipments. Whether tracking a product from the manufacturer to the consumer or keeping an eye on a fleet of vehicles, this system provides a complete view of the entire supply chain. When explicitly focused on vehicles, it's called a vehicle tracking system.

Types of Transport Tracking

Here is a quick overview of the methods of transport tracking.

GPS Tracking:

Global Positioning System (GPS) tracking uses satellites to identify the accurate location of shipments. In global logistics, such as FedEx's international freight movements, this technology enables monitoring and managing goods' transportation across borders.

SIM Tracking:

SIM tracking leverages mobile devices' Subscriber Identity Module (SIM) for shipment tracking within robust cellular network regions. Regional logistics enterprises use this to effectively monitor local deliveries, making it a suitable choice for short-range tracking.

RFID Systems:

Radio-Frequency Identification (RFID) systems use electromagnetic fields to identify and track tags attached to objects automatically. In logistics, like Amazon warehouses, RFID is applied to manage inventory by following pallets, crates, and individual parcels.

Smartphone Apps & Location Sharing:

Employing smartphone apps and location-sharing technology lets smaller logistics operations offer customers real-time tracking. This technology, used by local courier services, delivers a user-friendly solution to monitor last-mile deliveries.

Fastag-Based Tracking - ULIP:

Fastag is an electronic toll collection system that employs RFID technology for hassle-free toll payments. Fastag allows real-time tracking and updates in long-haul trucking logistics, enhancing highway travel efficiency.

Click on Check-in Links and Reminders:

This approach sends check-in links and reminders to drivers to ensure compliance with schedules and routes. In logistics operations, these digital tools can support on-time deliveries by reminding drivers to follow planned timelines.

Telematics:

Telematics consists of telecommunications and informatics integrations to deliver insights into vehicle conditions, route planning, and fuel efficiency. Several market players in logistics use this technology to analyze and optimize various aspects of fleet management.

Pros and cons of Transport Tracking

Here are a few pros and cons of the transport tracking system.

Pros

Enhanced Visibility:

Real-time tracking consolidates different stages of the supply chain, making it more effortless to adapt to unexpected changes. This visibility builds trust between stakeholders and authorizes more agile operations.

Improved Efficiency:

Efficiency improvements expand beyond cost savings to include faster response times to issues and predictive maintenance. It also streamlines the ability to fast adapt to disruptions, such as traffic or weather conditions.

Increased Safety:

Safety features can also incorporate automatic alerts for potential mechanical failures, leading to proactive maintenance. This reduces the risk of accidents, protects assets, and ensures the well-being of drivers.

Customer Satisfaction:

Real-time updates improve communication with customers, leading to greater trust and loyalty. Transparency in tracking strengthens the customer-brand relationship, giving them confidence in your service.

Compliance and Reporting:

The automation in tracking and reporting streamlines administrative tasks and reduces human error. It ensures that the business stays in line with continuously evolving regulations and standards.

Cons:

Implementation Challenges:

Besides complexity, inappropriate implementation can result in compatibility issues with existing systems. Thorough review and customization may be required, increasing the time and resources required for successful integration.

Cost:

Costs can increase further if the system needs continuous updates or modifications to comply with changing industry standards or regulations. Additionally, unforeseen technical issues may result in additional support and maintenance expenses.

Privacy Concerns:

These concerns lead to potential legal challenges and reputational risks if privacy is breached. Appropriate measures must be in place to familiarize all parties involved and to secure sensitive data, adhering to legal obligations and ethical considerations.

Common Mistakes in Transport Tracking Implementation

Transport tracking is crucial in the logistics industry for ensuring efficiency, safety, and cost-effectiveness. However, there are common mistakes that can hinder the success of implementing a tracking system. Here are some of the most frequent errors:

1. Lack of Understanding the Technology

If you don't fully grasp how a technology works, you might choose a system that doesn't suit your needs, like a GPS system that fails in areas with poor satellite coverage. Adequate research and expert consultation can guide you to the right solution.

  • Example: A small courier company opting for a high-end GPS system that fails in remote rural areas where they primarily operate.
  • Avoidance: Consulting with a technology expert to select a GPS system with offline capabilities would better suit their operational areas.

2. Ignoring Privacy Concerns

Overlooking privacy when using tracking technologies can result in legal troubles. Transparency and obtaining proper consent help maintain the privacy rights of those being tracked.

  • Example: Implementing smartphone-based tracking without considering driver privacy may lead to legal challenges.
  • Avoidance: Transparent communication with drivers and proper consent can prevent these issues.

3. Inadequate Training

Without proper training, staff may misuse technology like RFID tags. Comprehensive training ensures that everyone knows how to use the system correctly.

  • Example: A retail warehouse experiencing lost inventory due to improper use of RFID scanners by untrained staff.
  • Avoidance: Organizing regular training sessions on RFID technology would improve accuracy and minimize losses.

4. Neglecting System Integration

Failing to integrate new technology with existing systems can cause inefficiencies. Planning for smooth integration is essential for the system to work seamlessly.

  • Example: A logistics firm facing delays due to a new Fastag system not integrating with their existing route planning software.
  • Avoidance: Collaborating with IT specialists for a fully integrated solution would enhance efficiency.

5. Failure to Update and Maintain the System

Without regular updates and maintenance, tracking systems can lose accuracy. Regularly scheduled maintenance keeps the system working properly.

  • Example: A taxi company's outdated SIM tracking system leading to inaccurate location data, causing customer dissatisfaction.
  • Avoidance: Establishing regular maintenance checks and updates would ensure consistent performance.

6. Relying Solely on One Tracking Method

Using only one method, like Wi-Fi-Based tracking, can lead to failures. A combination of methods allows for consistent tracking across different scenarios.

  • Example: Using only Wi-Fi-Based tracking without a backup can lead to tracking failures in areas without Wi-Fi.
  • Avoidance: A combination of tracking methods tailored to various scenarios can provide more reliable and consistent tracking.

7. Overdependence on Network-Based Solutions

Depending entirely on network tracking in unstable areas can lead to data loss. Using diverse tracking methods ensures consistent coverage.

  • Example: A shipping company losing critical tracking data during a network outage in a remote area using only SIM tracking.
  • Avoidance: Combining SIM with satellite tracking would ensure reliable coverage in various terrains.

8. Ignoring Hardware Compatibility

Without considering hardware compatibility, a tracking system might not perform as expected. Evaluating these needs beforehand can save both time and resources.

  • Example: A trucking company finding its new GPS system incompatible with their vehicles' existing hardware.
  • Avoidance: Conducting a hardware compatibility assessment before purchase would prevent such issues.

9. Lack of Cost Analysis

Cost analysis is critical while choosing a tracking system. A detailed cost analysis ensures that the system meets your budget.

  • Example: A small delivery service investing in a costly Satellite-Based Vehicle Tracking System that exceeds their budget.
  • Avoidance: Performing a cost-benefit analysis to find an affordable yet effective tracking method like GPS or SIM tracking.

10. Neglecting Real-Time Tracking Needs

Inappropriate tracking methods, like using RFID for long-haul transportation, might not deliver necessary real-time data. Ensuring the tracking method aligns with transportation needs is essential.

  • Example: A long-haul trucking company unsuccessfully using RFID tracking, which is ideal for confined areas like warehouses.
  • Avoidance: Opting for GPS tracking, designed for long-range, would provide the real-time data needed for their operations.

11. Failure to Implement Security Measures

Without robust security, tracking systems like Wi-Fi-Based tracking could be hacked. Implementing robust security measures protects the data.

  • Example: A regional logistics firm experiencing a breach in its Wi-Fi-Based Vehicle Tracking System, causing interception of valuable shipments as the hackers gained precise location details. This leads to significant financial losses and damages the firm's reputation.
  • Avoidance: Deploying advanced cybersecurity measures, including encrypted communications, regular system audits, and intrusion detection systems, would prevent unauthorized access and safeguard critical data.

12. Inaccurate Usage of Hybrid Systems

Lack of knowledge in integrating technologies like GPS and cellular tracking, hybrid systems may extend challenges. Understanding the synergy between technologies ensures the system's efficiency.

  • Example: A logistics company responsible for transporting goods tried to save costs by blending GPS and cellular tracking. However, they failed to integrate the technologies, leading to gaps in tracking. As a result, a shipment went untracked for 12 hours, causing panic and potential loss of trust with their client.
  • Avoidance: Before implementing, the company should have sought expert advice on how to effectively combine the strengths of both GPS and cellular systems, ensuring uninterrupted tracking.
